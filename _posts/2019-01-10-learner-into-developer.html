A short history of me the learner

In highschool, I studied a CS subject for one year. I hated it! Studying all of this CS high-level theory stuff was not what I was expecting from a coding subject.

I dropped out at the end of the year and didn't looked back. Decided to go into maths/finance instead.

Forward to 3rd year of my Maths degree (2017), I wanted to give programming another chance. But didn't know where to start.

So I decided to start from everywhere. I started reading books and taking courses on Visual Studio, Android, Java, PHP, SQL, WordPress development, Python and PyGame (because why not create games in Python?).

Damn, I had no idea what I was supposed to do. Stuff had to change quickly, because summer internship application were already open.

Define your end goal

Do you want to

  • change careers?
  • get your first software developer internship?
  • become an indie maker?
  • code as a hobby?

Think why you want to code. Once you know how important coding is for your career (and life, let's be honest), then you can decide how many hours per day/week you should dedicate on becoming a software developer.

Follow people who are already there

Start following people who are already where you want to be. Twitter and Reddit are great places for this, where plenty of people can give you a daily dose of solid advice and motivation.

I am still the new kid on the block, but you can follow me on Twitter - I document my journey of being a self taught full-stack developer and making personal projects. Hope I can give you the extra motivation you need!

Pick specific stack and stick to it (for now)

If you want to be a front-end developer, focus on the things that will make you a front-end developer - HTML, CSS, design, React (or other frameworks).

If you want to be a mobile developer, focus on react-native or Swift/Android.

Massive shortcut to making the transition from learner to developer is not wasting time on things you will never use.

Remember all the languages I listed above? I spent months on courses and books, but I remember exactly nothing from them.

Make peace with yourself that it is ok not to know everything. Dan Abramov, the co-creator of create-react-app and the creator of Redux, doesn't know bunch of things. And it seems that he is fine with that.

Focus on the things that matter.

It's time to leave the tutorial paradise

Tutorials are great in the beginning, but at some point they become counter productive.

So many times I find a tutorial for an awesome project, I start watching it, but halfway through, I realise that I have just been copypasta from the video without actually learning anything.

But that's not the biggest problem. There is no danger in tutorials - they are perfect examples, where nothing goes wrong.

95% of development is problem solving!

Tutorials are great for learning the basics of new language/framework or when you are stuck on a problem, but they will only get you so far. To go beyond tutorials, you need to start building projects.

Coding == making things

Once you start creating things, you will find watching tutorials just for the sake of watching tutorials so boring.

In my opinion, creating projects is the catalyst to becoming a developer. And even once you are a developer, it's an instrument that keeps the passion going.

My opinion on development changed once I started working on my own little projects. All of the sudden, I wasn't following someone's tutorial - instead, I was on my own, making decisions on every single line of code and solving every little puzzle along the way.

Before I got my first software developer internship, I was making projects all the time. Even though they were basic projects, I still got the thrill of figuring out every problem thrown at me.

Code, bug, Google, fix, repeat. Personal projects are the best simulator to what it's like being a full-time developer as well as teaching you extremely valuable skills.
